Your VO2max of 51.6 predicts a 3:25-3:30 marathon for a fully trained runner, but your current volume of ~13 mi/week places actual race fitness closer to 4:30-5:00. The gap between aerobic capacity and structural readiness is the central challenge of this plan.

The SF course profile adds 5-7 minutes over a flat equivalent due to sustained climbing through Golden Gate Park and the out-and-back across the bridge.

Key insight: Your aerobic engine is strong but your running chassis is undertrained.

Phase 1

BASE
BUILDING

Weeks 1–5 · 15→22 mi/wk

The only goal of this phase is to build your aerobic foundation and let your connective tissues adapt to running volume. Every run is Zone 2 (HR 116-136). If your heart rate creeps above 136, slow down or walk.

This will feel embarrassingly slow — probably 12:00-13:00+ per mile. That is correct. Your cardiovascular system is ahead of your structural readiness, and running faster now risks shin splints, Achilles tendinopathy, and stress fractures. Strides (short 20-second accelerations) are the only intensity permitted.

Phase 4

TAPER

Weeks 15–17 · 30→14 mi + Race

The taper reduces volume by ~25% per week while maintaining intensity in short, sharp sessions. Research shows a well-executed 2-3 week exponential taper improves marathon performance by an average of 5 minutes and 32 seconds. Maintain running frequency — your body needs the routine. You will feel restless, heavy, and possibly irritable. These are normal "taper crazies." Trust the process.

Front-Loaded Course

54% of the climbing happens in the first half. The Golden Gate Bridge approach (mile 6–7), the return climb at mile 12, and Golden Gate Park (miles 16–18) demand patience. Run by heart rate and effort, never by GPS pace.

GPS pace is meaningless on hills. Your watch will show 10:30/mi on uphills and 7:30/mi on downhills — both are correct if your heart rate is in the right zone.

Segment Strategy

Miles 1-3: The Embarcadero

Flat
HR 130-138 8:50-9:15/mi

Deliberately conservative. You will feel amazing and fresh — resist the urge to go faster. Start at the slow end of target range. Take first gel at mile 3.

Miles 4-8: Fort Mason, Marina, Golden Gate Bridge

Climbing
HR 138-148 9:00-9:40/mi

Fort Mason hill is short but steep — shorten stride, lean from ankles. Real climb begins mile 6 up to bridge at 200 ft. Do NOT attack it. On the bridge expect wind and fog — tuck behind other runners. Gel at mile 7.

Miles 8-13: Marin Headlands and Back

Rolling hills
HR 138-148 Variable pace

Descent to Fort Baker is seductive — do NOT over-stride. Quick, short, soft steps. Mile 12 climb back to bridge gains 185 ft in one mile (~3.5%). Keep HR under 152. Walk briefly if needed at steepest section. Gel at mile 10.5.

Miles 13-18: Presidio and Golden Gate Park

Sustained climbing
HR 140-150 Accept slow pace

You've completed 54% of climbing. Mile 15 descent (225 ft) — use practiced technique: forward lean, quick turnover, soft landing. Then GG Park climb miles 16-18: sustained 3-4% grade that feels endless. Half-marathon runners with fresh legs will pass you — ignore them. Gels at miles 14 and 17.

Miles 18-22: Haight-Ashbury and Mission

Steep descent then flat
HR 140-148 Gravity assist

Haight-Ashbury descent at mile 20 is the course's most quad-damaging section. Your eccentric conditioning pays off here. High cadence, midfoot landing. After Market Street, relatively flat. Should feel tired but functional. Gel at mile 20.5.

Miles 23-24: Mariposa Street Hills

THE CRUX
HR 145-155 9:30-10:30/mi

This is where the race is decided. Short but devastating hills on depleted legs. Take caffeinated gel at mile 22. Baby steps on uphills. Power-walk steepest 30-second sections if HR spikes above 165. "This is the last hard thing."

Miles 24-26.2: Flat Finish

Flat, push it
HR 148-160+ Go for it

No more hills. Flat along the waterfront past Chase Center and Oracle Park. If you paced correctly, push now. Pick off runners who went too fast. Sprint final 0.2 if you have anything left. Finish under the Bay Bridge.

Hill Conditioning

Hill Programming

From zero hill training to SF-ready in 17 weeks

01 Familiarization Weeks 3-5
  • Gentle 2-4% inclines within easy runs, 5-10 min total per session
  • Walk all downhills. No structured repeats.
  • Goal: introducing calves, Achilles tendons, and hip flexors to incline loading
  • Your PPL training gives muscular strength but not tendon stiffness for repetitive hill running
02 Structured Introduction Weeks 6-8
  • Short hill repeats: 5x30 seconds progressing to 4x3 minutes
  • Grades of 4-6% on treadmill or moderate outdoor hills
  • Walking descents transition to slow jogged descents
  • First gentle downhill exposure (-3 to -5%) for 5-10 minutes
  • Eccentric strength exercises (step-downs, slow Bulgarian split squats) start at bodyweight
03 Race-Specific Building Weeks 9-13
  • Long hill repeats at tempo effort simulating GG Bridge and Golden Gate Park climbs
  • Structured downhill repeats (3x1.5 min at -5 to -6%, moderate-fast pace)
  • Triggers the Repeated Bout Effect — eccentric exercise protects against future muscle damage for up to 6 months
  • Course-simulation workouts combine sustained climbs with tempo efforts
  • Eccentric strength exercises progress to weighted loads
04 Maintenance & Inoculation Weeks 14-16
  • Hill volume within long runs but no new hill-specific sessions
  • One final downhill "refresher" in week 16 (2 weeks before race) to maintain RBE protection
  • Even 10 eccentric contractions provide significant protection

The Repeated Bout Effect

A single bout of eccentric exercise (like downhill running) protects against future muscle damage for up to 6 months. This is why the plan includes structured downhill repeats in weeks 9-13 and a final "refresher" in week 16. By race day, your quads will be pre-conditioned for the Haight-Ashbury descent and the Mariposa hills — the sections that destroy unprepared runners.

Bulgarian Split Squat

3x8-10 per leg, 3-5 sec eccentric

Primary quad and glute builder. Directly transfers to uphill power and downhill resilience.

Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

3x8-10 per leg

Hamstring and hip stability. Mimics single-leg stance phase of running.

Single-Leg Calf Raise

3x12-15 per leg, full ROM, 20-25 kg DB

Calves generate 55% of propulsive force in running.

Eccentric Step-Down

3x8-10 per leg, 4-5 sec descent

Directly simulates downhill quad loading.

Copenhagen Plank

3x20-30 sec per side

Hip adductor and abductor strength prevents knee collapse.

Dead Bug

3x10 per side

Anti-extension core stability for maintaining form in late miles.

Exercises to Drop

Chest flies, lateral raises, bicep curls, tricep extensions, leg extensions, leg curls. Keep 1-2 light sets of push-ups and rows for upper-body maintenance if desired, but hypertrophy work adds mass you'll carry for 26.2 miles. Every extra kilogram costs ~2 seconds per mile.

Scheduling Rule

Schedule strength on easy-run days to consolidate stress and preserve quality on hard-run days. Lift in the afternoon/evening if you run in the morning (6+ hour gap ideal). Never lift heavy the day before a long run, tempo, or hill session.

Carb Loading Protocol (3 Days Before Race)

Thursday — 3 days out

Target 10-12 g/kg = 788-946g carbs

White rice, pasta, bread, juice, sports drinks. Low fiber. Big lunch, moderate dinner.

Friday — 2 days out

Same carb targets

Make lunch the biggest meal. Lay out race gear. Charge GPS watch. Set 3 alarms.

Saturday — 1 day out

Reduce volume slightly

Easy dinner by 6 PM. Prepare drop bag. Sleep by 9 PM. Pre-bed snack: cereal or toast with honey at 8 PM.

Pre-Race Meal

Pre-race meal 3 hours before start: 2 bagels with jam + banana + 16 oz sports drink (~150-200g carbs). Tested foods only. This is not the time to experiment.

Safety

Risk Management

The single greatest threat to your marathon is not fitness — it's injury

Daily Monitoring

Every morning before getting out of bed, check resting heart rate and HRV on your wearable. Track 7-day rolling averages, not single readings.

Red Flags

RHR elevated 5+ bpm (≥54) for 3 consecutive days

→ Cut week's mileage by 30%, drop all quality sessions

HRV trending downward for 5+ consecutive days

→ Take extra rest day, reduce next long run by 20%

Sharp or localized pain during running

→ Stop immediately. 2-3 days off. Physio if persists after 3 days.

Easy-run HR consistently 10+ bpm above normal at same pace

→ You are under-recovered. Take 2 rest days.

Persistent fatigue, mood disturbance, or sleep disruption for 3+ days

→ Step-back week regardless of plan position.

Mileage Adjustment Rules

Never increase weekly mileage by more than 10% above highest recent non-step-back week.

Miss 5+ days (illness/injury): resume at 70% of pre-break volume, rebuild over 2 weeks.

Miss 10+ days: restart at beginning of current phase.

Missing one long run is manageable. Missing two consecutive: peak long run drops 2 mi, shift to B/C goal time.

Shoe Strategy

  • At 78.8 kg, replace shoes every 300-350 miles (not typical 400-500)
  • Rotate between 2 pairs to vary mechanical loading
  • Break in race-day shoes by week 12 with at least 30 miles on them
  • Consider cushioned daily trainer (Hoka Bondi, ASICS Gel-Nimbus, NB Fresh Foam) for easy runs, lighter shoe for quality

Critical Injury-Risk Windows

Weeks 5-7

First significant mileage jump. Connective tissue under novel load.

Weeks 9-11

High volume + quality introduced simultaneously.

Week 14

Peak mileage (40 mi). Maximum cumulative fatigue.

During these windows, be extra vigilant about recovery: sleep 8+ hours, eat sufficient protein (1.6-2.0 g/kg/day = 126-158g daily), and foam roll 10-15 minutes after every run. If something feels 'off,' default to rest. One skipped workout is invisible in your fitness; one injury can cost you the entire race.
